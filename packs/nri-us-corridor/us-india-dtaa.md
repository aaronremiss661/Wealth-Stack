# India-US DTAA Treaty Rates
**Last updated**: 2025-03-25
**Source**: India-US Double Taxation Avoidance Agreement (1989, amended)
**Target agent**: nri-advisor

## Treaty Overview

The India-US DTAA was signed in 1989 and has been amended via protocol. It governs how income is taxed when earned cross-border between India and the US.

## Key Treaty Rates

| Income Type | India Rate | US Rate | Treaty Rate | Relief Method |
|------------|-----------|---------|-------------|---------------|
| Dividends | 10-20% | Ordinary rates | 15% (25% if >10% holding) | Credit in US |
| Interest | 10% TDS | Ordinary rates | 15% | Credit in US |
| Capital gains (shares) | 12.5% LTCG / 20% STCG | US rates | Taxed in BOTH countries | Foreign tax credit |
| Capital gains (property) | 12.5% LTCG | US rates | Taxed in BOTH countries | Foreign tax credit |
| Royalties/FTS | 10% | US rates | 15% | Credit |
| Pension | Taxed in India | Taxed in US | Taxed in residence country | Credit |
| Salary | Where services rendered | Worldwide | Country of service | Credit |

## Key Articles

- **Article 10 (Dividends)**: Max 15% withholding in source country (25% for >10% ownership)
- **Article 11 (Interest)**: Max 15% in source country
- **Article 13 (Capital Gains)**: Taxable in both countries — use foreign tax credit to avoid double taxation
- **Article 15 (Employment)**: Taxed where services are performed
- **Article 25 (Relief from Double Taxation)**: US gives credit for Indian taxes paid; India gives credit for US taxes paid

## Claiming Treaty Benefits in India

1. Obtain Tax Residency Certificate (TRC) from US (IRS Form 6166)
2. Submit Form 10F to Indian deductor/tax authority
3. File ITR in India claiming Section 90 relief (DTAA credit)
4. Report Indian income on US return (Form 1040 + Schedule B/D)
5. Claim Foreign Tax Credit on US return (Form 1116)

## Common Pitfalls

- **PFIC rules**: Indian mutual funds are classified as PFICs by the IRS — punitive US tax treatment. Avoid holding Indian MFs as a US tax resident.
- **Capital gains**: No special treaty rate — taxed fully in both countries, rely on foreign tax credit
- **NRE interest**: Tax-free in India but MUST be reported and taxed in the US
- **PPF/EPF**: US does not recognize as pension — interest is currently taxable in the US
- **Gift from India**: No gift tax in India for NRI recipients, but US has reporting requirement ($100K+ from foreign person → Form 3520)

## Optimal Structure for US NRIs

1. Hold US investments in US accounts (401k, IRA, brokerage) — simpler tax treatment
2. Limit India investments to: direct equity (avoid PFIC), NRE FDs (tax-free in India), real estate
3. Avoid Indian MFs (PFIC issue) — use US-listed India ETFs (INDA, SMIN) instead
4. Use NRE account for repatriable investments, NRO for Indian rental income
5. File ITR in India every year if Indian income exceeds basic exemption
